# The Problem

## Knowledge is fragile

Teams today struggle with a fundamental problem: knowledge is fragile and context is ephemeral.

Decisions are made in meetings that leave no trace. Design choices are recorded in documents that fall out of date. The reasoning behind a piece of code, a product direction, or an architecture call lives in someone's head and disappears when they leave the room.

Documents exist, but they decay. PRs are merged, issues closed, and the knowledge that produced them disappears. What remains is the output. The reasoning behind it is gone.

## Agents make it worse

AI agents have made this worse in one specific way: every time you invoke an agent, it starts from zero. It has no memory of what your team decided last week, no understanding of why things are the way they are, no stake in the outcome.

The result is that teams are getting faster at execution but no better at accumulating knowledge. The more agents you add, the more context you lose.

## Context routing

When agents have no shared memory, a specific pattern emerges. We call it _context routing_.

You open a session with an agent. You spend the first ten minutes explaining: here is our stack, here is why we chose a monolith over microservices, here is who owns what, here is what we tried last quarter that did not work.

The agent produces something. It is decent, but it misses context. It does not know about work that another teammate or another agent already did. You correct. You re-explain. You paste in documentation.

Then you open a second session with a different agent. That agent also knows nothing. You explain again. You copy context from one session to another, manually.

The human becomes the sole carrier of organizational context, manually ferrying it between agents that cannot see each other. The more agents you add, the more routing work you do.

You added agents to move faster. Instead you added a job.

## The gap is not intelligence

The bottleneck is not capability. These models can write code, reason about architecture, debug production issues. They are genuinely good.

The bottleneck is _memory_.

Think about what makes a senior engineer effective. It is not that they are 10x smarter than a junior. It is that they carry _context_. They know the history of the codebase. They know which approaches were tried and abandoned. They know who owns what and why things are the way they are. That _accumulated context_, built up over months, is what makes them fast.

AI agents have none of this. Every session is a blank slate. You can give them a `CLAUDE.md` file or a system prompt, but that is a flat document. A cheat sheet. It does not capture the relationships between decisions. It does not update itself when things change. It does not scale.

The gap between a great human teammate and an AI agent is not intelligence. It is context.

Context Tree closes that gap.
